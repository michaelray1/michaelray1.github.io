Computational Astrophysics



My current work is in the field of computational astrophysics, where I have been working with Dr. Rosalba Perna and her collaborators. I am working on two projects at current.

The first project is investigating possible explanations for why we see hot jupiters in our universe. Hot jupiters are jupiter-like planets (large and gaseous planets) which orbit very close to their host stars. When these were first discovered in 1995, they were quite a surprise to astronomers, because our current theory of planet formation predicts that planets as large as jupiter cannot form much closer to their sun than where our jupiter orbits. So, if we are to accept our current planet formation theory, then we must explain how these hot jupiters could have gotten to where they are without initially forming there. The theories we are interested in posit that a some of these hot jupiters could have formed far out in their solar system and then migrated inwards due to an external body such as another star perturbing the orbits of the planets in the system.

The second project in this field that I am involved in has to deal with how radiation is affected as it travels through a dense volume of space, such as near the center of a galaxy. In these very dense environments, light that is emmitted from a source gets changed quite a bit before it reaches our telescope. There are a variety of things that could happen to the light, such as it could be absorbed by a Hydrogen molecule, which could then emit another photon. Since there are so many things which could happen to the light on its way to our telescope, it is sometimes quite tricky for astronomers to figure out what emmitted the light that we observe. My project here is to use a radiative transfer code to figure out what our telescopes would observe if gamma rays were emmitted in the environment of an active galactic nuclei.



Quantum Field Theory



Under the supervision of Dr. Philip Argyres, I have worked on a number of problems in quantum field theory and mathematical physics. I began work with Dr. Argyres in the spring of 2020 and have continued to work with him since then. The problem we are focused on currently has to do with understanding the space of lowest energy states of N=4 Supersymmetric Yang-Mills theories. This class of theories contain artificial symmetries which are not observed in nature, but which allow us to get a much better hold on the predictions of the theory. Studying these theories will help physicists to better understand our true physical models of the world such as the standard model of particle physics.

Cosmology



My very first research experience in physics came back in the spring of 2018 under the supervision of Dr. Colin Bischoff. I continued work with Dr. Bischoff until the spring of 2020. This work was in the field of cosmology. Dr. Bischoff and I worked on developing a filter for CMB data which was meant to provide a reconstructed map of the CMB in which we only keep the divergence-free component of the light. These divergence-free components are called B-modes and they contain information about the early universe. Of particular interest to Dr. Bischoff's work is the fact that inflationary gravitational waves, if they existed in the early universe, would leave an imprint on the CMB in the form of B-modes. Therefore, obtaining an estimate of the B-mode signal is of importance for answering the question of whether inflation happened in our universe.

Unfortunately for us, the B-mode signal in the CMB is very faint compared to the E-mode signal (this is the curl-free component of the light). In addition, when telescopes (such as the BICEP/Keck array at the South Pole) are connected to the Earth, they only observe a portion of the sky. Measurements of polarized light are local measurements, though. Because of this, near the edge of the telecope's observing area, we cannot unambiguously distinguish between E-modes and B-modes. This is where our filter comes in. Our filter iteratively solves for the Wiener filter reconstruction of our data. That is, our filter outputs the map which minimizes chi-squared, or equivalently, the error in our measurement. You can see a demonstration of this in the figure below. It shows that our filter resulted in a lower variance (error bar size) when compared to the S2-hat estimator, which is currently used in the BICEP/Keck analysis pipeline.

The code developed for this project is open source and available on my GitHub account here. Also in that GitHub repository is a jupyter notebook which gives a demonstration of how the code works. A logbook posting in the CMBS-4 collaboration which was authored by Dr. Bischoff and myself can be found here. A poster for this work can be seen below.



Metric Space Geometry



During my senior year at UC, for my mathematics capstone, I spent a year working with Dr. David Herron in the math department at UC on a project in metric space geometry. Metric space geometry is a field of mathematics concerned with how we measure distances on a set of points. There is the distance that everybody learns about in middle school math, that is, the euclidean measure of distance. There exist more exotic notions of distances, however. Some of these notions are very practical, like the taxi-cab distance. This is a measure of distance using the distance that a taxi would have to drive in order to get to a destination. Some of the notions of distances are yet more exotic and do not have as obvious of a use in the real world. One of these distances is called the Ferrand distance. Dr. Herron and I worked on obtaining estimates for the Ferrand distance between two points by using only properties of the ambient metric space itself. We were able to set upper and lower limits on the Ferrand distance using quantities such as the diameter of the boundary of our metric space. This work we performed was based on a previous study that Dr. Herron did, here in which he and his collaborators considered very similar questions about a different notion of distance, called the quasi-hyperbolic distance.
